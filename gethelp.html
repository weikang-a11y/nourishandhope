Finding help for an eating disorder can feel overwhelming. This page gives you everything you need in one place — helplines, how to find a provider, what to expect from treatment, and how to navigate insurance.

Understanding treatment

Levels of care explained

Eating disorder treatment isn't one-size-fits-all. There are different levels depending on how much support is needed — your treatment team will recommend the right one.

Outpatient (OP)

1–3 sessions per week

Weekly appointments with a therapist and/or dietitian. Best for early-stage EDs or as a step-down from more intensive care. You continue living at home and going to school.

Intensive Outpatient (IOP)

3–5 days per week, 3 hrs/day

More structured support including group therapy, meals with support, and individual sessions. You still live at home but have more daily support.

Partial Hospitalisation (PHP)

5 days per week, 6–8 hrs/day

A full-day program with meals, groups, and therapy — but you go home each evening. Provides intensive structure without inpatient admission.

Residential / Inpatient

24/7 care

Around-the-clock medical and psychological care in a treatment facility. For cases where safety requires constant monitoring. Typically a short-term bridge to lower levels of care.

Practical guidance

Navigating insurance

The cost of eating disorder treatment can feel daunting. Here's how to advocate for coverage.

🛡️ Know your rights

Under the Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act, insurance plans must cover mental health and eating disorder treatment at the same level as physical health conditions. This means if your plan covers physical therapy, it must cover eating disorder therapy too.

Here's how to advocate for the coverage you're entitled to:

1
Call your insurance company and ask specifically: "What eating disorder treatment benefits do I have, and what is covered at each level of care?"
2
Ask your treatment provider if they are "in-network" — and if not, ask about "single case agreements" where out-of-network providers can sometimes be covered.
3
If a claim is denied, file an appeal. Many denials are overturned on appeal, especially when your treatment team provides a letter of medical necessity.
4
Contact your state's insurance commissioner if you believe your rights are being violated. Many states have mental health parity enforcement offices.
5
If you have no insurance, ask treatment centers about sliding scale fees, payment plans, or state-funded mental health programs in your area.
First steps

What to expect when you first reach out

Not knowing what happens next can be one of the biggest barriers to seeking help. Here's exactly what the first steps look like.

Step 1

First call or email

You contact a helpline, provider, or your doctor. You'll be asked some basic questions about what's going on. You don't need to have all the answers — just share what you can.

Step 2

Initial assessment

A clinician will do a more thorough assessment — asking about eating patterns, history, physical health, and emotional wellbeing. This is not a test. There are no wrong answers.

Step 3

Treatment recommendation

Based on the assessment, a level of care will be recommended. You'll have the chance to ask questions and share your concerns before any decisions are made.

Step 4

Building your team

You'll be connected with the right providers — usually a therapist, a dietitian, and a doctor working together. You don't have to manage multiple appointments alone — your team coordinates.

Step 5

Treatment begins

Treatment starts at whatever pace makes sense. Your team will check in regularly and adjust the plan as you progress. Recovery is a collaboration — you have a voice in your own care.

You've already taken the hardest step.

Being here, reading this, looking for help — that takes courage. Whatever comes next, you don't have to figure it out alone. Reach out today.
