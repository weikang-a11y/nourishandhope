Clearing up misconceptions

Myths vs facts

Eating disorders are widely misunderstood — even by doctors, teachers, and family members. Here's the truth.

✗ Myth

"Eating disorders only affect thin, white teenage girls."

✓ Fact

Eating disorders affect people of all genders, ages, races, body sizes, and backgrounds. Someone can be in a larger body and still have a serious eating disorder.

✗ Myth

"It's just a phase. They'll grow out of it."

✓ Fact

Eating disorders are serious mental health conditions with significant physical health consequences. They rarely resolve on their own and almost always require professional support.

✗ Myth

"Parents cause eating disorders by being too controlling or too lenient."

✓ Fact

Eating disorders have complex, multi-factor causes. Parents do not cause them. In fact, family involvement is one of the most powerful factors in a child's recovery.

✗ Myth

"If they're eating, they can't have an eating disorder."

✓ Fact

Many eating disorders — like bulimia, binge eating disorder, and ARFID — involve eating. The disorder is in the relationship with food, not just the amount consumed.

✗ Myth

"They could just eat normally if they really wanted to."

✓ Fact

Eating disorders involve real changes in brain chemistry and thought patterns. Willpower alone cannot fix them, any more than willpower can fix diabetes or a broken arm.

Know the difference

Types of eating disorders

Each eating disorder has its own patterns, experiences, and treatment needs. Here's an overview of the most common types.

🌿 Anorexia nervosa

Characterized by severe restriction of food intake, intense fear of weight gain, and a distorted body image. Can involve compensatory behaviours like excessive exercise.

Has two subtypes: restrictive (limiting food only) and binge-purge (restriction plus episodes of bingeing and purging).

Most commonly emerges in adolescence, but can affect any age.

🌊 Bulimia nervosa

Involves cycles of binge eating followed by compensatory behaviours like vomiting, laxative use, or excessive exercise. Often driven by shame and a desire to "undo" eating.

Many people with bulimia maintain an average body weight, making it less visible than anorexia.

Often begins in late adolescence or early adulthood.

🌀 Binge eating disorder

Recurrent episodes of eating large amounts in a short period, with a feeling of loss of control, followed by distress, guilt, or shame — but without purging behaviours.

The most common eating disorder in the US. Often goes unrecognised and untreated.

Affects people across all age groups and genders equally.

🎯 ARFID

Avoidant/Restrictive Food Intake Disorder involves avoiding foods based on sensory characteristics (texture, smell, appearance) or fear of choking/vomiting — not related to body image.

Can lead to nutritional deficiencies and significant impairment in daily life.

More common in children, but can persist into adulthood.

✨ OSFED

Other Specified Feeding or Eating Disorder is the most common eating disorder diagnosis. It covers presentations that cause significant distress but don't fully meet criteria for the above categories.

OSFED is just as serious as other diagnoses and deserves the same level of care.

Affects people of all ages, genders, and backgrounds.

🔍 Orthorexia

An obsession with eating "purely" or "healthily" that becomes so extreme it interferes with daily life, relationships, and physical health. Not yet an official diagnosis but widely recognised clinically.

Can masquerade as health-consciousness, making it difficult to identify.

Increasingly common in wellness-focused cultures and social media users.
What we know

What causes eating disorders?

Eating disorders don't have a single cause. They develop from a complex mix of biological, psychological, and social factors — and no single person or thing is to blame.

🧬

Genetics

Research shows eating disorders run in families and have a significant genetic component. Having a relative with an ED increases risk.

🧠

Brain chemistry

Differences in serotonin, dopamine, and other neurotransmitters play a role in how people experience hunger, reward, and mood around food.

💭

Psychological factors

Perfectionism, low self-esteem, anxiety, trauma, and difficulty managing emotions are strongly associated with eating disorder development.

📱

Social & cultural pressure

Diet culture, social media, and societal ideals about body size can trigger and maintain disordered eating — especially in adolescence.

🏡

Life experiences

Trauma, bullying, difficult life transitions, or comments about weight and food — especially during childhood — can be contributing factors.

⚖️

Dieting

Restrictive dieting — especially in young people — is one of the strongest risk factors for developing an eating disorder. The body's response to restriction can trigger ED behaviours.

Important: Understanding causes helps with compassion — not blame. Whether you're a parent, a young person, or someone supporting a loved one, knowing the causes helps you respond with care rather than judgment.

There is a way forward

What does recovery look like?

Recovery is not a straight line — but it is real, and it happens for thousands of people every year. Here's what the journey typically involves.

🌱

Recognition — something is wrong

Recovery usually begins with someone recognising that the relationship with food is causing harm — whether that's the young person themselves, a parent, a friend, or a doctor. This step alone takes enormous courage.

🤝

Assessment — understanding what's happening

A doctor or specialist assesses the physical and psychological situation. This determines what level of care is needed — from weekly outpatient therapy to more intensive support.

🧠

Treatment — working with a team

Effective eating disorder treatment usually involves a team: a therapist, a dietitian, and a doctor working together. Therapy approaches like CBT, FBT (Family-Based Treatment), and DBT are evidence-based and proven to work.

📈

Progress — two steps forward, one step back

Recovery rarely goes in a straight line. There will be hard days, setbacks, and moments of doubt. This is normal and expected — not a sign of failure. What matters is continuing to show up.

🌟

Freedom — a life beyond the eating disorder

Full recovery means being able to eat without constant fear or guilt, to feel at home in your body, and to not have food consume your mental energy. This is achievable — and it's what recovery looks like for many people.
