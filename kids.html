Understanding what's going on

What is an eating disorder?

Eating disorders aren't really about food. They're about feelings — pain, fear, control, or numbness — that end up getting tied to eating. Here's what different ones can feel like.

Anorexia nervosa

Anorexia involves eating very little — not because you're not hungry, but because eating feels scary or wrong. It often comes with an intense fear of gaining weight and a distorted view of your own body (seeing yourself as bigger than you are).

It's not vanity or a diet gone too far. It's a serious condition where the brain becomes trapped in a cycle of restriction, fear, and distorted thinking — and it needs real support to break out of.

💛 You might have anorexia if you find yourself skipping meals most days, counting every calorie, feeling terrified of certain foods, or feeling like you can never be thin enough no matter what the mirror or scale says.

Bulimia nervosa

Bulimia involves cycles of eating large amounts of food (bingeing) followed by trying to "undo" it — by vomiting, using laxatives, or exercising excessively. It's usually driven by shame, stress, or feeling out of control.

Many people with bulimia look completely "normal" from the outside, which makes it feel invisible and isolating. But what's happening inside — the shame spiral, the secrecy — is exhausting and painful.

💛 You might relate to bulimia if you feel like you "lose control" around food sometimes, and then feel intense guilt or panic afterward and try to compensate.

Binge eating disorder

Binge eating disorder means eating large amounts of food in a short time, often feeling like you can't stop, even when you're not hungry. Unlike bulimia, it doesn't involve purging — but it comes with enormous shame and distress.

Binge eating is often a response to difficult emotions — loneliness, anxiety, boredom, or pain. Food becomes a way to cope, even though it leaves you feeling worse.

💛 This is one of the most common eating disorders and one of the least talked about. If this sounds familiar, know that it's real, it's recognised, and it's treatable.

ARFID (Avoidant/Restrictive Food Intake Disorder)

ARFID involves avoiding many foods — not because of body image worries, but because of things like texture, smell, fear of choking or getting sick, or just having a very limited range of "safe" foods.

It's not pickiness. It's a genuine difficulty that can make eating stressful and social situations around food feel impossible.

💛 ARFID is more common than people think, especially among kids and teens who may also have sensory sensitivities or anxiety. It's not your fault and it can get better with the right support.

Other eating disorders

There are other eating disorders too — like orthorexia (obsession with "clean" or "healthy" eating), pica (eating non-food items), and rumination disorder. There's also OSFED (Other Specified Feeding or Eating Disorder), which is the most common diagnosis and simply means your symptoms don't fit neatly into one category.

Whatever you're experiencing — if your relationship with food feels painful, scary, or out of control — it deserves attention and care, whether or not it has a name.

💛 You don't need a diagnosis to deserve help. If eating feels hard and it's affecting your life, that's enough reason to reach out.
Questions you might have

Honest answers to real questions

These are things lots of young people wonder but might feel too embarrassed to ask out loud.

Is this actually an eating disorder, or am I just being dramatic?

+
If your relationship with food is causing you pain, stress, or getting in the way of your life — that's real and it matters. You don't have to be at a certain weight or severity to deserve help. The fact that you're asking this question at all is worth paying attention to.

Do I have to tell my parents?

+
You don't have to do anything before you're ready. But we'd gently encourage you to think about telling at least one trusted adult — a parent, relative, school counselor, or doctor. Not because you're in trouble, but because getting better is really hard to do alone, and you deserve support. If you're nervous about your parents' reaction, a school counselor can sometimes help you figure out how to have that conversation.

Will I have to gain weight / be forced to eat things I hate?

+
Good treatment isn't about forcing you to do things. It's about working with a team you trust to help your body and mind feel safe again — at a pace that works for you. A good therapist and dietitian will listen to your fears and work with you, not against you.

What if I don't want to recover?

+
That feeling is more common than you'd think — and it makes sense. An eating disorder can feel like it's helping you cope, giving you control, or even protecting you somehow. It's okay to feel ambivalent about getting better. You don't have to want recovery fully to start talking to someone. You just have to be a little curious about what life could be like.

Can I recover completely, or will I always struggle with this?

+
Full recovery is absolutely possible — and it happens for many people. Recovery looks different for everyone, and it's rarely a straight line. But people go on to eat freely, feel at peace with their bodies, and not think about food all day. That future is available to you.

My friend might have an eating disorder. What should I do?

+
The most important thing you can do is be honest and gentle. Tell them you've noticed something, that you care about them, and that you're not going anywhere. Don't make it about food or weight — make it about them. If you're worried they're in danger, tell a trusted adult. It might feel like a betrayal, but it could save their life.
