For parents & caregivers

You are not failing your child.

Watching your child struggle with an eating disorder is one of the most frightening and isolating experiences a parent can face. You are not alone — and your involvement is one of the most powerful forces in their recovery.

Early recognition

Warning signs to watch for

Eating disorders often develop quietly. Knowing what to look for helps you act early — and early action leads to dramatically better outcomes.

🍽️ Around food & eating

  • Skipping meals or making excuses not to eat
  • Obsessing over calories, ingredients, or "clean" eating
  • Eating very small portions or refusing whole food groups
  • Disappearing to the bathroom after meals
  • Hoarding or hiding food
  • Eating large amounts secretly or very rapidly

🧍 Physical signs

  • Noticeable weight loss or fluctuation
  • Feeling cold all the time, even in warm weather
  • Dizziness, fainting, or low energy
  • Hair thinning or loss
  • Swollen cheeks or jaw pain
  • Calluses on knuckles (from purging)

💭 Behavioural & emotional

  • Intense fear of gaining weight
  • Distorted body image ("I'm so fat" when clearly not)
  • Withdrawing from friends and activities they used to love
  • Excessive exercise even when injured or exhausted
  • Extreme mood swings, especially around mealtimes
  • Wearing loose clothing to hide body shape

Remember: You don't need to see every sign to be concerned. Trust your instincts as a parent. If something feels wrong, it's worth talking to a doctor — even if you're not sure. Acting early is never the wrong choice.

Starting the conversation

How to talk to your child

The way you start the conversation matters enormously. These steps help you open a door — not start a fight.

1

Choose the right moment

Don't bring it up during or right after a meal — emotions are already heightened. Find a calm, private moment when you're both relaxed, like during a walk or a car ride where there's no eye contact pressure.

2

Lead with love and observations, not accusations

Focus on specific behaviours you've noticed and how they make you feel — not on food, weight, or their body. This keeps them from becoming defensive.

"I've noticed you seem really stressed lately and you haven't been eating with us much. I'm not angry — I'm just worried because I love you and I want to make sure you're okay."
3

Listen more than you speak

Your child may deny everything, get angry, or cry. That's okay. Your goal in this first conversation isn't to solve anything — it's just to let them know you see them and you're not going anywhere.

"You don't have to tell me everything right now. I just want you to know I'm here and we'll figure this out together, whatever it is."
4

Avoid these phrases

Even with the best intentions, some things can make your child shut down or feel worse.

✗ "You look fine to me — you're not that thin."
✗ "Just eat something, it's not that hard."
✗ "Why are you doing this to our family?"
✗ "I didn't raise you to be like this."
5

Seek professional help together

After the conversation, make an appointment with your child's doctor or a specialist — and frame it as something you're doing together, not something being done to them.

"I'd like us to go talk to someone who understands this better than I do. Not to get you in trouble — just so we can get you the right support."

Practical guidance

Navigating the healthcare system

Getting the right help can feel overwhelming. Here's a clear map of what to expect and how to advocate for your child.

👨‍⚕️

Start with your pediatrician

Your child's regular doctor is usually the first step. They can assess physical health, rule out other conditions, and refer you to specialists.

🧠

Find an ED specialist

Look for therapists, psychologists, or psychiatrists who specialise specifically in eating disorders — not just general mental health.

🥗

Registered dietitian

An ED-specialised dietitian works alongside therapists to help rebuild a healthy relationship with food — without diet culture thinking.

🏥

Levels of care

Treatment ranges from weekly outpatient therapy to intensive day programs to inpatient care. Your team will recommend the right level.

📋

Insurance & costs

Eating disorder treatment is often covered under mental health parity laws. Ask your insurer specifically about ED treatment benefits.

📍

Find providers near you

Use our provider directory to search for ED-specialised therapists and dietitians in your area, filtered by insurance and age group.

Take care of yourself too.

You cannot pour from an empty cup. Supporting a child with an eating disorder is emotionally exhausting — your wellbeing matters and directly affects your ability to help.

🧘 Set aside guilt

You did not cause this. Eating disorders are complex conditions — not the result of bad parenting.

💬 Talk to someone

Find your own therapist or support group. Caregiver burnout is real and deserves attention.

🌙 Protect your basics

Sleep, movement, and connection with people who support you are not luxuries — they're necessities.

🤝 Accept help

Let others in your life help with meals, errands, or childcare. You don't have to carry this alone.
